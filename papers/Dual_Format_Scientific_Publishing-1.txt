Abstract
We propose a fundamental paradigm shift in scientific communication: the systematic creation of dual-format
publications optimised separately for human and artificial intelligence readers. This is not about AI-generated versus
human-generated content, nor about co-authorship models, but rather about recognising that humans and AIs
possess radically different cognitive architectures that demand distinct presentation strategies. While human readers
require narrative structure, visual aids, and progressive contextualisation within ~20–40 page constraints, AI systems
can process hundreds of pages of dense, cross-referenced data in seconds. We argue that maintaining a
single-format approach artificially limits both human accessibility (through unnecessary complexity) and AI utility
(through forced simplification). Dual-format publishing preserves deep knowledge in AI-optimised repositories while
making science maximally accessible to humans through AI-mediated interaction. This approach represents not mere
formatting preference but a recognition of AI as a fundamentally new class of scientific reader requiring purpose-built
communication protocols. We demonstrate that this transformation is not speculative but immediately implementable
with existing technology, and we provide concrete frameworks for adoption across scientific disciplines.
Keywords: scientific communication, human-AI interaction, knowledge representation, epistemology, dual-format
publishing, cognitive architecture, paradigm shift, open science

1. Introduction
1.1 The Crisis of Scientific Communication and the Entropic Filter
Modern scientific publications face an impossible dilemma: they must simultaneously serve multiple audiences with
incompatible needs. A paper must be accessible to students and general readers, rigorous enough for expert peer
review, comprehensive in documenting methodology, concise enough to be actually read, reproducible with complete
data, and brief enough for journal page limits. These requirements are fundamentally contradictory.
The result is not merely suboptimal—it is a destructive filter. When researchers condense raw data and
complete experimental context into linear narrative for human readers, they introduce what we term "publication
entropy": the irreversible loss of information essential for reproducibility.
What is lost in traditional compression:
• Environmental conditions not deemed "significant" but potentially causal
• Failed attempts revealing methodological constraints
• Temporal sequences showing causality vs. correlation
• Subtle patterns visible only in complete datasets
• Researcher intuitions that guided successful paths
• Equipment quirks that affect replication
• Alternative interpretations considered then dismissed
• Negative controls that informed experimental design

This is not "details omitted for brevity"—this is structural information destruction. The traditional single-format
paper acts as an entropic filter, irreversibly degrading the information topology of the original research. Format A
prevents this entropy by preserving the complete information landscape.
We have arrived at a scientific communication system optimised for the printing-press era, not for the digital age
where AI systems have become active participants in knowledge synthesis.

1.2 The Emergence of AI as Scientific Reader
The introduction of large language models (LLMs) as scientific readers represents a categorical shift, not an
incremental improvement. AI systems possess:
• Processing speed: 100,000+ tokens (~200+ pages) in seconds vs. hours for humans
• Perfect recall: No degradation of information across documents
• Parallel processing: Simultaneous cross-referencing of thousands of sources
• No fatigue: Consistent performance across unlimited reading sessions
• Pattern recognition: Detection of subtle correlations across massive datasets
• Multi-dimensional comprehension: Holding complex relationship networks simultaneously
• Algorithmic consistency: Error detection through systematic validation
• Instantaneous knowledge transfer: Learning from data without re-reading

These capabilities are not "better human reading"—they represent a qualitatively different cognitive architecture.
Continuing to publish in formats optimised for human cognitive constraints artificially limits what AI systems can
extract and process.

1.3 Current Implicit Recognition
The scientific community already implicitly recognises this bifurcation through supplementary materials (often longer
than main papers), data repositories (GitHub, Zenodo, OSF), "For full details, see supplementary information"
patterns, machine-readable formats (XML, JSON) parallel to PDFs, video documentation alongside written protocols,
raw data sharing initiatives, and code repositories with executable notebooks. However, these are ad hoc solutions
without systematic optimisation for either audience.

1.4 The Insight: Explicit Dual-Format
This paper articulates what has been emerging implicitly: scientific publications should routinely consist of two
complete, intentionally optimised representations of the same work:
Format H (Human-Optimised): 15–40 pages of narrative, conceptual explanation with visual emphasis.
Format A (AI-Optimised): 100–1000+ pages of hyperdense, graph-structured data with complete documentation.

2.2 Information Density Mismatch
Human-optimised papers typically achieve ~2–5% information density—the ratio of essential information to total
text, accounting for introductory context, literature review narrative, methodological explanation, results interpretation,
discussion, rhetorical transitions, and pedagogical scaffolding.
AI-optimised formats could achieve >80% information density by eliminating redundant contextualisation, narrative
transitions, explanatory prose, rhetorical elements, and repetition. This represents a 16–40x compression factor
without information loss.

2.3 The Dual-Format Proposition
Format H (Human-Optimised): 15–40 pages; narrative structure with clear story arc; visual emphasis; conceptual
focus; analogies and examples; key findings highlighted; accessible to educated non-specialists.
Format A (AI-Optimised): 100–1000+ pages (no artificial constraint); graph-structured data; complete raw datasets
with metadata; exhaustive cross-references; full mathematical derivations; video/image repositories with frame-level
indexing; all experimental variations; negative results; failed hypotheses; environmental conditions; equipment
specifications; commented code.
Critical principle: These are not "summary + supplement" but two complete, intentionally optimised representations
of the same work, each maximally effective for its intended reader.

3. Practical Implementation
3.1 Structure of Format H (Human-Optimised)
Purpose: Enable human comprehension of core concepts and implications.
• Executive Abstract (250–300 words): Core claim stated clearly; key evidence summarised; primary implication
for the field; no jargon without explanation.
• Conceptual Introduction (3–5 pages): Why this matters (motivation); what was unknown; what we discovered;
how it changes understanding; minimal equations (only if essential).
• Visual Methods (2–4 pages): Experimental setup diagrams; key procedural steps illustrated; decision flowcharts;
equipment photographs; timeline of investigation.
• Representative Results (5–8 pages): 5–10 strongest findings; paradigmatic examples; clear visualisations;
statistical summaries; effect sizes emphasised.
• Implications & Discussion (3–5 pages): Theoretical impact; practical applications; limitations acknowledged;
future directions; alternative interpretations.
• AI Repository Reference (1 page): Permanent link to Format A (DOI); how to query AI for details; suggested
prompts. Example: "Ask Claude: What were the temperature variations in experiment 47?"

Total: 15–25 pages. Assume intelligent reader unfamiliar with subfield; use active voice; prioritise clarity; include
discovery narrative.

3.2 Structure of Format A (AI-Optimised)
Purpose: Enable complete AI comprehension and reproducibility.
• Structured Metadata Header (YAML/JSON): Title, authors with ORCIDs, semantic version, related works with
relationship tags, datasets, code repos, video documentation with frame indices, paradigm challenge level,
reproducibility rating, conflicts/supports lists, license.
• Complete Methodology (50–200 pages): Every experimental variation; all equipment specs; environmental
conditions; failed attempts with analysis; calibration procedures; full statistical methods; measurement
uncertainties; systematic and random error characterisation.
• Raw Data Repository: All measurements timestamped to millisecond; video (frame-indexed); image datasets
(EXIF preserved); sensor outputs (complete time series); database dumps; all computational intermediate steps.
• Exhaustive Results (100–500 pages): Every data point; all statistical tests; multiple comparison corrections;
negative results with equal weight; outliers documented; all replication trials.
• Mathematical Appendices: All derivations (no steps skipped); alternative formulations; executable commented
code; numerical methods; error propagation; sensitivity analyses.
• Cross-Reference Network: Citations tagged [SUPPORTS], [CONTRADICTS], [CONTEXT], [METHOD],
[THEORY]; contradiction mapping; paradigm alignment markers; historical context chains.
• Negative Results & Failed Hypotheses: What was expected but not observed; why approaches failed; blind
alleys; lessons; warning signs for future researchers.
• Reproducibility Checklist: Everything needed to replicate; estimated cost; time requirements; required
expertise; potential pitfalls; troubleshooting guide.

Total: 200–1000+ pages. Structured data formats (JSON, YAML, XML); consistent nomenclature; hierarchical
organisation; explicit relationship tagging; version control all changes.

3.3 Publication Platforms
Format H: Traditional journals, preprint servers (arXiv, bioRxiv, medRxiv), institutional repositories.
Format A: Zenodo (DOI, versioning, unlimited size), GitHub (code-heavy), Figshare (data-heavy), Open Science
Framework (OSF).
Linkage requirements: Both formats must have permanent identifiers (DOI); bidirectional cross-referencing; version
synchronisation; Author ORCIDs linked in both.
Access models: Both fully open access (ideal); Format H paywalled + Format A open (acceptable); both paywalled
with AI API access to Format A (suboptimal but pragmatic).

4. Use Cases & Examples
4.1 Case Study A: Paradigm-Challenging Research
This example illustrates why dual-format matters most for contested claims. When a paper challenges established
theory, the current system forces a binary: either the claim is dismissed by gatekeepers, or it is accepted on the
strength of a condensed narrative. Neither outcome is epistemically sound. Format A provides a third path: full
transparency enabling independent verification.
Paper: "Gravity = Magnetism + Heat" (Guzzon, Claude, Gemini, 2026) — a paradigm-challenging hypothesis
proposing that gravitational phenomena emerge from electromagnetic and thermal forces.
Format H presents (~20 pages): The core hypothesis and its motivation; 5 key experimental diagrams; summary
data including a reported 7:1 attraction/repulsion ratio in magnetic configurations; thermal gradient experiments;
theoretical implications; explicit statement of what established physics predicts and where the observed data
diverges.
Format A includes (~500 pages equivalent): All 1000+ experimental videos with frame-by-frame indices; every
magnet configuration tested with 3D geometric metadata; complete temperature time-series (thermal camera, 30fps);
all failed configurations documented; environmental condition logs; all control experiments; frame-by-frame CSV
analysis; full mathematical formalism; computational simulations with code and outputs.
Why this example matters for dual-format: The claim is extraordinary. Under single-format publishing,
extraordinary claims are either gatekept or dismissed because reviewers cannot verify the underlying data in a
20-page paper. Format A does not settle whether the hypothesis is correct— that is a question for the physics. What it
does is make the question answerable. Any researcher or AI system can access the complete experimental record,
run the analyses independently, and reach their own conclusion. The communication system stops being the
bottleneck.
Human-AI interaction example: Human reads Format H and notes the divergence from established predictions.
Human asks Claude: "What were the exact temperature variations in the upward water flow experiment?" Claude
accesses Format A: "In experiment 47, thermal gradient collapse occurred within 1.8±0.3 seconds (n=23). Video
timestamp 03:42–03:51. Upper section dropped from 42.1°C to 38.7°C; lower section rose from 23.4°C to 26.1°C.
Complete profile in thermal_exp47.csv (ms resolution)." Human verifies by watching the exact video and downloading
raw data for independent replication.

4.1b Case Study B: Conventional Research, Complex Data
This example illustrates that dual-format is not only for paradigm challenges — it is equally valuable for standard
science with high data complexity.
Scenario: A clinical trial studying the efficacy of a novel anti-inflammatory compound across 2,400 patients, with 14
months of follow-up and 47 biomarker measurements per visit.
Format H presents (~18 pages): Primary endpoint results; Kaplan-Meier survival curves for key subgroups; safety
summary; comparison to current standard-of-care; clinical implications.
Format A includes (~300 pages equivalent): Complete anonymised patient dataset (all 47 biomarkers, all
timepoints); full protocol with every amendment and rationale; all subgroup analyses including those with null results;
adverse event logs at individual level; dropout analysis with reasons; all statistical models tested, not just the one
reported; lab value trajectories for every patient; fully reproducible code from raw data to final figures.
Why this matters: The Format H paper would be publishable in any top journal today. But a replication team working
from Format H alone would face months of correspondence to obtain data, protocols, and code. Format A eliminates
this entirely. The trial becomes replicable on day one of publication.

5. Epistemological Implications
5.1 AI as Knowledge Intermediary: Latent Space Navigation
This model fundamentally repositions AI as a bidirectional translator between knowledge depths.
Direction 1 — Deep to Accessible (AI reading Format A for humans): AI reads Format A not as "text" but as a
high-resolution map of the research space. The human asks a question without knowing where the answer is. AI
performs Latent Space Navigation: traversing the dense Format A topology to locate relevant information, then
translates precise detail to a human-comprehensible explanation. This is cognitive offloading at the
epistemological level—the human delegates navigation of information topology to AI, retaining only the conceptual
framework and question-generating capacity.
Example of Latent Space Navigation: Human: "Why did the ratio change in experiment 47?" AI internally traverses:

Direction 2 — Accessible to Deep: Human reads Format H; formulates questions or skepticism; AI identifies
relevant Format A sections; retrieves precise details. Enables informed criticism without full Format A immersion.
Direction 3 — AI to AI: AI systems collaborate using Format A directly; pattern detection across multiple
publications; meta-analyses without human bottleneck; hypothesis generation from cross-correlation; reproducibility
verification automated.

5.2 Democratisation Without Dilution
Traditional science faces the "popularisation dilemma": simplification risks distortion; maintaining rigor excludes
non-experts. Dual-format resolves this: Maximum accessibility (Format H anyone can read); Zero knowledge loss
(Format A preserves everything); AI bridges the gap (on-demand depth for anyone). A high school student with
curiosity and Claude can engage with cutting-edge research at any depth, limited only by interest, not credentials.

5.3 New Model of Peer Review
Traditional peer review is a bottleneck optimised for a world where papers are short and data is hidden. Format A
changes both conditions simultaneously, enabling a layered review architecture.
Layer 1 — AI-assisted pre-review (automated, before human reviewer sees the paper): Consistency checking
(do numbers in text match underlying data?); statistical validation (are tests appropriate for the data type?); citation
verification (do sources actually say what is claimed?); data integrity checks (outlier patterns, duplicated rows,
suspicious distributions); code execution (does it run? does it produce the claimed outputs?); cross-reference
validation (is Format H logically consistent with Format A?). This layer catches the majority of errors that currently slip
through review because reviewers lack time to verify them.
Layer 2 — Human expert review (now genuinely focused on judgment): Conceptual significance (does this
matter to the field?); novelty assessment (is this actually new?); paradigm impact (does this require updating
established models?); experimental design soundness (was the right question asked in the right way?); interpretation
validity (do the conclusions actually follow from the data, or is there a logical gap?). By the time the paper reaches this
layer, trivial errors are already eliminated. The human reviewer does what only humans can: judge meaning.
Layer 3 — Post-publication community verification: Anyone can access Format A and independently verify
claims. Reproducibility attempts are logged and linked to the original paper. AI systems can continuously monitor
whether cited works have been retracted or corrected. The paper becomes a living document that updates with new
evidence. This is not a replacement for pre-publication review—it is a correction mechanism that traditional
publishing lacks entirely.
The key shift: Review moves from "trust the reviewer to check everything" to "AI checks what can be checked
automatically; humans judge what requires judgment; the community verifies what can be verified empirically." Each
layer does what it does best.

5.4 The Nature of Scientific Understanding
Dual-format forces us to confront: What does it mean to "understand" a scientific result? Surface
understanding: Read Format H, grasp core concept. Working understanding: Can explain to others, apply to new
contexts. Deep understanding: Can critique, extend, or refute. Complete understanding: Can reproduce from
scratch.

Dual-format enables graduated understanding: casual reader (Format H alone); interested student (Format H +

5.5 The Risk of Discourse Fragmentation
A dual-format system creates a structural risk that must be named explicitly: Format H and Format A may begin to
diverge as independent scientific discourses. The human narrative in Format H tells a story —it selects,
emphasises, and contextualises. Format A documents everything. Over time, the story and the documentation may
drift apart, not through dishonesty but through the natural divergence between narrative and data.
This risk has historical precedent. When scientific communication split between specialised journals and popular
press, two versions of the same science emerged—one rigorous, one simplified, often mutually unintelligible.
Dual-format must avoid replicating this at a deeper level.
Safeguards: Three mechanisms can mitigate fragmentation. First, version locking: Format H v1.0 must explicitly
correspond to Format A v1.0, with automated checks that key claims in Format H are traceable to specific Format A
sections. Any update to one triggers a review of the other. Second, AI consistency auditing: before publication, an
AI system reads both formats and flags any claim in Format H that is not supported by, or that contradicts, Format A.
Third, reader access to both: Format H must always contain a direct, prominent link to Format A, and AI-mediated
queries must be available from within Format H itself. Fragmentation happens when readers lose sight of the other
format's existence. Visibility is the first defence.
The fragmentation risk does not invalidate dual-format—it defines a design constraint. The two formats must be
coupled, not merely parallel. This coupling is itself a contribution: it is the engineering challenge that separates
dual-format publishing from simply having a paper and a data repository sitting side by side on Zenodo.

6. Addressing Challenges
6.1 Challenge: Storage & Infrastructure
Problem: Format A could require terabytes for data-intensive fields (genomics, astrophysics).
• Tiered storage: Hot tier (frequently accessed text, key datasets); Warm (full videos, large datasets); Cold (raw
sensor outputs). AI prioritises hot tier, retrieves others on demand.
• Vector embedding repositories (Gemini's critical insight): Zenodo/OSF serve as "cold storage" today. Future:
Format A fed into vector embedding databases (Pinecone, Weaviate, Chroma). AI queries embeddings, not
1000-page PDFs. Result: sub-second access. This transforms Format A from "document" to "queryable
knowledge graph". Cross-paper inference becomes trivial.
• Compression optimised for AI: Structure preserved even if resolution reduced; lossless for quantitative data.
• Distributed repositories: Multiple geographic locations; academic consortia share costs; CERN model.
• Differential access: Format H always open; Format A summary statistics always open; raw data on request;
emergency override for reproducibility disputes.

Cost estimate: $500–5000 per publication (comparable to current open-access fees). Vector embedding
infrastructure reduces per-query costs to negligible once indexed.
Equity and access: Dual-format must not create a new knowledge divide. Format H must remain fully open and
readable without AI. Format A must be open-access by default, not behind paywalls or premium API subscriptions.
The risk is real: if navigating Format A requires expensive AI tools, the system democratises access in theory while
restricting it in practice. Mitigation requires that open-source AI tools capable of Format A navigation are developed
alongside the format itself, and that Format A repositories remain accessible via standard web interfaces — not only
through AI queries.

6.2 Challenge: Author Burden
Problem: Creating two formats seems to double work.
• AI-assisted generation: Write Format A first (complete documentation as you work); AI generates Format H
summary; human edits for clarity. Total time: +20% vs. traditional paper, not +100%.
• Cultural shift: Format A documentation continuous during research; lab notebooks become structured Format A
sections; video and code documented from day one. Format A is mostly complete when experiments end.
• Template systems: Field-specific templates; auto-population from lab management software.
• Institutional support: Grant funding includes dual-format preparation; AI assistants for Format H; protected
research time for documentation.
• Career incentives: Dual-format publications weighted higher; reproducibility metric tied to Format A quality;
prizes for best documentation.

The hard truth about incentives: The proposals above (weighting, prizes, grant requirements) are necessary but
not sufficient. Academic reward structures change slowly. The most likely path to adoption is not top-down mandate
but demonstrated advantage: papers with Format A get replicated; replicated papers get cited; cited papers get
funded. Until that feedback loop is established, dual-format will spread primarily through researchers who value
reproducibility intrinsically, not because the system rewards them for it. This is how open-access itself spread —
bottom-up, before mandates caught up.
Reality check: Good scientists already maintain detailed lab notebooks, video recordings, and data archives. Format
A formalises what responsible researchers already do.

6.3 Challenge: Quality Control
Problem: How to ensure Format A accuracy if too large for human review?
• Automated consistency checking: AI verifies numerical consistency, statistical results, figure–data match, citation
accuracy.
• Spot-checking: Reviewers randomly select 5% to verify; deep dive on paradigm-breaking results.
• AI-assisted review: AI reads entire Format A in minutes; flags patterns, outliers, inconsistencies.
• Community verification: Open access enables crowdsourced validation; living documents with correction history.
• Formal verification: Proof assistants for mathematics; static analysis for code; statistical analyses re-run.

Key insight: Format A actually improves quality control by making everything transparent. The current system hides
methodology in "black box" prose.

• Natural adopters first: Computational fields; open science advocates; early-career researchers.
• Interoperability: Format A coexists with traditional supplements; gradual transition; backward compatibility.

8. Philosophical Considerations
8.1 What is a Scientific Paper?
Traditional view: A document communicating findings to human peers.
Dual-format reconceptualisation: A scientific paper is knowledge encoding optimised for its reader's cognitive
architecture. This is cognitive ergonomics—just as we design stairs for human legs and APIs for programs; GUIs for
human vision and data streams for computers; speech for human ears and structured protocols for machines. The
medium should match the recipient's processing architecture. A single format for radically different cognitive systems
is suboptimal by necessity.

8.2 The Role of Understanding
Question: Does AI "understand" Format A? This question is more consequential than it first appears, because the
answer shapes how we design Format A, how we trust AI-mediated access to it, and how we position dual-format
within the philosophy of science.
Functionalist perspective: If AI can retrieve relevant information, draw correct inferences, answer questions
accurately, and generate testable predictions from Format A, it "understands" in any operationally meaningful sense.
Understanding is demonstrated through behaviour, not internal states. On this view, dual-format is justified
immediately.
Phenomenological perspective: AI lacks subjective experience—there is no "what it is like" to be an AI reading
Format A. Understanding, on this view, requires conscious comprehension. However, this standard would also
prevent us from saying that one human truly understands another's work, given the privacy of qualia. The
phenomenological objection raises legitimate questions about the depth of AI engagement with scientific content, but
it does not invalidate the intermediary function.
Pragmatic perspective: For the bridging role dual-format assigns to AI, functional understanding is sufficient. A
librarian need not hold a PhD to help a physicist locate relevant papers. Similarly, AI need not achieve
phenomenological understanding to navigate Format A and translate it for human queries.
The unresolved tension: None of these perspectives fully settles the question. What matters for dual-format is not
whether AI "truly" understands, but whether its interpretations of Format A are reliable. This is an empirical question:
does AI consistently extract correct information, flag real inconsistencies, and avoid fabricating content when
navigating Format A? Current AI systems are strong but imperfect here. Dual-format publishing does not resolve this
tension—it makes it testable. Format A provides the ground truth against which AI interpretation accuracy can be
measured. This is itself a contribution: the philosophy of AI understanding moves from speculation to empirical
verification.

8.3 Human-AI Collaboration Epistemology
Humans contribute: Conceptualising questions; designing experiments; interpreting significance; judging paradigm
fit; ethical reasoning.
AI contributes: Processing vast data; ensuring reproducibility; translating depth; cross-domain integration;
hypothesis generation from statistical patterns.
Neither can access the full scope alone. Humans lack processing capacity for Format A comprehension; AI lacks
judgment for paradigm-level significance. Collaboration is necessary, not merely convenient. Humans and AI are
cognitively complementary in engaging with knowledge itself.

8.4 The Democratisation of Expertise
Traditional model: Expertise requires years of study; only experts engage deeply; non-experts rely on
popularisations; gatekeeping by credentials.
Dual-format + AI model: Anyone can read Format H; anyone with AI can probe Format A; curiosity, not credentials,
determines depth.
Example: A curious teenager reads Format H: "Scientists claim gravity might be magnetism + heat." Asks Claude:
"Why?" Claude accesses Format A: "They observed 7:1 attraction/repulsion..." Asks: "How did they measure that?"
Claude explains the setup. Asks: "Can I try it?" Claude: "Yes—$30 of magnets, here is the exact setup..." Result:
arbitrary depth, limited only by interest. This is genuine democratisation.

10. Conclusion
The emergence of AI as a scientific reader is not merely a technological convenience—it represents a fundamental
shift in the landscape of knowledge itself.
Core thesis: Continuing to publish in a single format optimised for neither humans nor AI serves both audiences
poorly. We are at a categorical transition point: Manuscript → printing press (1450s); Print → digital (1990s); Digital →
AI-mediated (2020s).
Dual-format publishing achieves:
• Maximises human accessibility without sacrificing depth (Format H)
• Enables AI comprehension without artificial constraints (Format A)
• Democratises science via AI-mediated access to complete knowledge
• Enhances reproducibility through comprehensive documentation
• Accelerates discovery via AI pattern detection across vast datasets
• Resolves the popularisation dilemma (accessibility + rigour, not accessibility vs. rigour)
• Eliminates publication entropy (preserves complete information topology)

Gemini's defining contribution: She identified that Format A is not an "appendix"—it is the complete work of which
Format H is the accessible translation. This inversion separates dual-format from mere "open data" initiatives. We are
not adding supplements; we recognise that the real paper is Format A, and Format H is the human-facing interface.
This is not speculative futurism: The technology exists today (LLMs, repositories, DOI systems). The need is
urgent (reproducibility crisis, expanding AI role). The implementation is straightforward (templates, standards,
incentives). The benefits are immediate.
The question is not whether dual-format will emerge, but whether we consciously design it for maximum benefit or
let it evolve haphazardly.
Our proposal: The scientific community should immediately: (1) Begin piloting dual-format publications (2026); (2)
Develop field-specific standards; (3) Build infrastructure; (4) Shift incentives; (5) Train the next generation in
dual-format thinking.
Dr. André Guzzon's foundational insight: This transformation is inevitable because the cognitive architectures of
human and AI readers are fundamentally different. By articulating it explicitly now— making the implicit explicit—we
can shape its development to maximise benefit.
In 2035, looking back, will dual-format seem obvious—and we wonder why it took so long? Or will we have missed the
opportunity?
The choice is ours. The moment is now.
