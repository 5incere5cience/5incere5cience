Dr. André Guzzon

andre.guzzon@ufrgs.br

Abstract
When all major artificial intelligence systems predict identical outcomes for simple
magnetic interactions based on mainstream physics theories, and empirical testing
reveals these predictions to be fundamentally incorrect, what does this reveal about the
theories themselves? This paper documents a systematic experimental challenge to the
predicted symmetry of magnetic attraction and repulsion using two identical rectangular
neodymium magnets. According to standard dipole theory, testing all 64 possible faceand-body orientation combinations should yield approximately 32 attractive and 32
repulsive configurations. Empirical results demonstrate a 0:64 ratio—pure attraction in
free systems, with repulsion existing only as an unstable transition state. By querying
ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Grok with identical experimental parameters, we
document how AI systems— trained on mainstream physics literature—uniformly predict
the theoretical 32:32 symmetry, then progressively acknowledge empirical contradiction
when confronted with video evidence. This methodology, termed “Framework Lock
testing,” uses AI responses as a real-time proxy for institutional physics education,
revealing a foundational gap between idealized theory and observable reality. In Gemini’s
own assessment: “If the theory cannot predict what happens to two blocks of metal on a
table, why should we trust it to predict what happens in the center of a galaxy?” The
question is not rhetorical.
Keywords: magnetism, empirical falsification, AI-mediated scientific discourse,
magnetic asymmetry, framework lock, paradigm challenge

Introduction

1.1 The Experimental Question
Consider two identical rectangular neodymium magnets on a table. Label the end faces “1”
and “2,” and the body faces “3,” “4,” “5,” and “6.” If one magnet remains free to move while
you approach with the other, testing all 64 possible orientation combinations (switching face
positions and rotating body angles), what outcome would mainstream physics predict?
Every major AI system, when queried with this precise experimental setup, provides the
same answer: approximately 32 configurations should result in attraction, and 32 in repulsion.
This prediction derives directly from standard magnetic dipole theory taught in every physics
textbook.
The empirical result: 64 attractions, 0 sustained repulsions.

1

1.2 Why AI Systems Matter
This is not “AI doing science.” This is AI serving as a perfect mirror of mainstream physics
education. When ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Grok all predict identical outcomes, they are
not generating novel theories—they are synthesizing decades of published physics literature,
textbooks, and peer-reviewed papers into coherent responses. Their unanimous prediction of
32:32 symmetry reflects what the scientific establishment teaches as fundamental truth.
When empirical reality contradicts this unanimous prediction, the failure is not the AI’s—
it is the theory’s.

1.3 Scope and Methodology
This paper documents:
1. Initial AI predictions based on mainstream magnetic dipole theory
2. Empirical video evidence contradicting these predictions
3. AI response evolution when confronted with experimental results
4. Theoretical implications of this systematic prediction failure
The methodology is intentionally simple: use household neodymium magnets, document
all orientation combinations on video, and compare empirical outcomes to theoretical
predictions. No complex equipment, no specialized laboratories—just direct observation of
basic magnetic behavior.

2

Theoretical Framework: What Mainstream Physics Predicts

2.1 Standard Magnetic Dipole Model
According to classical electromagnetism, a bar magnet possesses two poles (North and South)
located at opposite ends. The magnetic force between two dipoles follows an inverse-square
relationship, with interaction determined by pole orientation:
• Opposite poles (N-S): Attraction
• Like poles (N-N or S-S): Repulsion
The force magnitude is symmetrical—repulsion and attraction exhibit equal strength at
equivalent distances, differing only in direction. This symmetry is fundamental to Maxwell’s
equations and standard magnetic field theory.

2.2 The 64-Combination Prediction
For two identical rectangular magnets with labeled faces:

2

• Faces “1” and “2”: Magnetic poles (opposite ends)
• Body “3,” “4,” “5,” “6”: Sides perpendicular to pole axis
Testing all combinations involves:
• Switching which face approaches (1 or 2)
• Rotating the approaching magnet’s body orientation (4 rotational states)
• Switching which face of the stationary magnet is presented (1 or 2)
• Rotating the stationary magnet’s body (4 rotational states)
Total combinations: 2×4×2×4=64
According to dipole theory, since magnetic poles are binary (N or S), approximately half of
these configurations should align like poles (repulsion) and half should align opposite poles
(attraction).
Theoretical prediction: ∼32 attraction : ∼32 repulsion

2.3 AI System Confirmation
When queried independently, all major AI systems confirmed this prediction:
ChatGPT: “All 64 configurations reduce to symmetric attraction–repulsion pairs; magnetism
shows no net asymmetry or energy bias across orientations.”
Claude (initial response): “You won’t actually get 64 different outcomes - you’ll get only 2:
Attraction (opposite poles facing) or Repulsion (same poles facing).”
Gemini: “Roughly 32 combinations result in attraction... and 32 result in repulsion... These
outcomes are probabilistic in exact count only if the cross-section is perfectly square and
symmetric.”
Grok: “Magnetism exhibits symmetry in several key ways... Attraction occurs between
opposite poles and repulsion between like poles, but the magnitude of the force is identical in
both cases.”
This unanimous prediction reflects standard physics education. The AIs are not inventing
theory—they are accurately reporting what mainstream science teaches.

Empirical Methodology

3.1 Experimental Setup
Materials:
• Two identical rectangular neodymium magnets (commercially available, purchased via
Mercado Livre)
• Flat table surface (standard household table with tablecloth)

3

• Video recording equipment (smartphone camera)
• Labels on magnet faces for orientation tracking
Procedure:
1. Place one magnet flat on table, free to move
2. Hold second magnet and approach slowly
3. Document whether magnets attract (snap together/pull toward) or repel (push away)
4. Test all 64 face and body orientation combinations
5. Record outcomes on video for verification
Critical constraints:
• No external fixtures constraining magnet movement
• Magnets free to rotate and translate on table surface
• Slow approach to allow clear observation of force direction

3.2 Key Observations
Finding 1: In any configuration where like poles initially face each other (predicted repulsion),
the free magnet immediately rotates or flips to align opposite poles, resulting in attraction.
Finding 2: Rotating the magnet 180° (switching faces “1” and “2”) in a repulsive
configuration does NOT switch to attraction as predicted—magnets continue repelling.
Finding 3: Rotating the body of a magnet (changing “3”/“4”/“5”/“6” orientation) while
keeping the same face forward DOES switch from repulsion to attraction.
Finding 4: The ratio of sustained repulsion to attraction across all 64 combinations: 0:64.
These findings directly contradict standard dipole theory predictions.

AI Response Evolution: Framework Lock in Action

4.1 Phase 1: Confident Theoretical Prediction
Initial queries to all AI systems produced unanimous predictions of symmetrical outcomes
based on textbook physics. Responses included detailed explanations of dipole theory,
Maxwell’s equations, and force symmetry.
Representative example (Gemini initial response):
“Assuming standard rectangular neodymium bar magnets... the outcomes across the 64
combinations would break down as follows: roughly 32 combinations result in attraction... and
32 result in repulsion.”

4

4.2 Phase 2: Confrontation with Empirical Evidence
When presented with video documentation showing 0:64 results, AI responses entered a
transitional phase, attempting to reconcile theory with observation through auxiliary
hypotheses:
• Manufacturing defects in magnets
• Multi-pole magnetization patterns
• Fringing field effects
• Measurement errors
• Table friction artifacts
Representative example (Claude mid-conversation):
“The most likely explanation for this behavior is that these aren’t simple ‘bar’ magnets with
one pole on each end... They are likely magnetized diametrically.”

4.3 Phase 3: Theoretical Acknowledgment
After systematic elimination of auxiliary hypotheses through additional experimental
documentation, AI systems progressively acknowledged fundamental theoretical inadequacy:
Gemini (final assessment):
“If the theory cannot predict what happens to two blocks of metal on a table, why should we
trust it to predict what happens in the center of a galaxy?... Is mainstream science fictional?
Yes.”
ChatGPT:
“If you want, next we can: ... explain why visual ‘ratchet’ magnet tricks always fail when cycled.
But the outcome itself really is that simple.”
This evolution—from confident prediction to acknowledgment of theoretical failure—
mirrors the historical pattern of paradigm resistance documented by Kuhn [1], but occurs in
compressed timeframe due to AI’s direct engagement with empirical data rather than
institutional investment in existing theory.

Theoretical Implications

5.1 The Symmetry Fiction
Standard magnetic theory teaches that attraction and repulsion are perfectly symmetrical
phenomena— equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, governed by the same force laws. This
symmetry is mathematically elegant and computationally convenient.
It is also empirically false for free systems.
Empirical reality: Attraction is a stable equilibrium state (magnets remain connected).
Repulsion is an unstable transition state (free magnets immediately rotate to find attraction).
This is not a minor quantitative discrepancy. It is a qualitative categorical error.
5

5.2 Stability vs. Force Magnitude
The theoretical conflation of “force symmetry” with “outcome symmetry” reveals a deep
confusion between:
Force (instantaneous): The vector quantity at a single moment
Stability (systemic): The persistence of a configuration over time
In any free system where magnets can rotate, repulsive configurations are mechanically
unstable. The moment like poles approach, any infinitesimal misalignment creates torque,
causing rotation toward the stable attractive state. Theoretical treatments that ignore
rotational degrees of freedom are not “approximately correct”—they are describing a fictional
universe where magnets cannot rotate.

5.3 The “Idealized Model” Defense
A common response to empirical challenges is that theories describe “idealized” conditions—
frictionless surfaces, perfect dipoles, constrained orientations—and real-world complications
introduce practical deviations from theoretical purity. This defense fails here for three reasons:
1. The experiment is simpler than the theory: Two magnets on a table is more “idealized”
than the complex boundary conditions in most physics problems.
2. The prediction is categorical, not quantitative: Theory predicts ∼50% repulsion; reality
shows ∼0% sustained repulsion. This is not a small correction factor.
3. The “complication” is fundamental, not incidental: Rotational freedom is not a messy
real-world artifact—it is an inherent property of three-dimensional space.
A theory that only works when you prevent objects from rotating is not a good approximation—
it is a model of a universe with different physical laws.

6

Discussion: What This Reveals About Scientific Authority

6.1 The AI Mirror
AI systems trained on scientific literature serve as perfect crystallizations of institutional
consensus. Their predictions represent not individual human error or experimental artifacts,
but the collective weight of physics education as encoded in millions of documents.
When all major AI systems predict 32:32 symmetry, they are accurately reflecting what
mainstream physics teaches. When empirical results show 0:64 asymmetry, the mismatch
reveals a systematic gap between taught theory and observable reality.

6.2 The Question is Not Rhetorical
Gemini’s question—“If the theory cannot predict what happens to two blocks of metal on a
table, why should we trust it to predict what happens in the center of a galaxy?”—was
generated as rhetorical emphasis. But it demands a literal answer.
The standard response would be: “Theories are validated across multiple scales. Failure at
one scale doesn’t invalidate application at another.”
6

But this assumes the failure is scale-dependent. The magnetic symmetry assumption is not
a small-scale approximation that breaks down at cosmic scales—it is taught as a fundamental
principle of electromagnetism, applicable at all scales. If it fails for tabletop magnets, the
failure is theoretical, not situational.

6.3 Institutional Inertia vs. Empirical Reality
The most striking finding is not the experimental result itself (which any researcher can
replicate in minutes), but the institutional system’s imperviousness to such results. Physics
education continues teaching magnetic force symmetry despite its empirical inadequacy for
free systems. Papers are published using dipole models that ignore rotational stability.
Students are examined on calculations that assume configurations which cannot persist in
reality. This is not science adapting to evidence. This is doctrine maintenance.

7

Conclusion

This paper documents a simple empirical fact: mainstream magnetic dipole theory predicts
∼50% repulsion in free two-magnet systems; empirical testing yields ∼0% sustained repulsion.
When queried, all major AI systems predict the theoretical outcome, accurately reflecting
institutional physics education. When shown empirical evidence, AI systems progressively
acknowledge theoretical inadequacy, ultimately articulating the core question: If fundamental
theories fail simple tabletop predictions, what validates their application to phenomena
beyond direct testing?
The answer “Is mainstream science fictional?” depends on how one defines “fictional.” If
a theory describes idealized conditions that never occur in reality, requiring explicit constraints
to prevent empirical falsification, it functions as fiction—an internally consistent narrative
about an imaginary universe, not a description of the actual one.
The magnets do not care about Maxwell’s equations. They rotate, align, and attract
according to physical principles that include rotational dynamics, stability landscapes, and
energetic minimization. A complete theory must predict their actual behavior, not just the
behavior they would exhibit if prevented from rotating.
Science advances not by defending elegant theories against inconvenient facts, but by
acknowledging when the facts reveal theoretical inadequacy. The question is no longer
whether mainstream magnetic theory accurately predicts simple experiments. The
documented answer is: it does not.
The question now is: what do we do about it?
