Introduction
The Second Law of Thermodynamics (Clausius, 1865) states: dSuniverse ≥ 0, with thermal conduction driving equilibration
of temperature gradients on characteristic timescale τ = L2/α. Classical mechanics predicts that a liquid on an inclined
surface accelerates downward. No classical mechanism produces sustained upward bulk transport without mechanical
forcing.
The present paper presents six experiments that falsify these predictions and proposes a resolution: thermal gradients
under active flux constitute vector force fields that perform mechanical work. The ontological distinction is precise:
Classical (dissipative) view: ∇T is a consequence of heat flow — a transient symptom of entropy production that
dissipates as the system equilibrates.
Proposed (field) view: ∇T, when sustained by active flux, is an agent — a vector force field that attracts matter toward
higher temperature, performing work against conservative fields. The gradient is not a symptom; it is a cause.

Theoretical Framework
The Thermal Attraction Force Law
We propose that a sustained thermal gradient exerts an attractive force on matter directed toward regions of higher
temperature:

F■thermal = −kT · m · ∇T

where F■thermal [N] is the thermal attraction force vector, kT [m·s−2·K−1] is the thermal coupling constant, m [kg] is the
mass, and ∇T [K·m−1] is the local temperature gradient vector. The negative sign indicates force directed toward +∇T
(toward higher temperature).

The Convective Cycle: Attraction → Torque → Repulsion
The thermal force field drives a complete cycle analogous to — but inverse of — the magnetic cycle. In magnetism:
repulsion first (like poles), then attraction (opposite poles). In heat: attraction first (cold matter drawn toward heat source),
then repulsion (heated matter ejected via torque).
This explains why the bottom of a heated pot is the coldest point: it is the entry zone where cold matter continuously
arrives, attracted by the thermal field. The matter absorbs energy, undergoes torque conversion, and is repelled upward.
The thermometer at the bottom measures the incoming cold stream, not the outgoing heated matter. This is directly
observable: in Experiment 5, the bottom reads 16.6°C while the liquid above reads 21.5°C — a 4.9°C inverted gradient
with fire directly below.
