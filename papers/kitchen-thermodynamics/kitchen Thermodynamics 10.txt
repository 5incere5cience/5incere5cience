Fine. Let’s test that.
What was tested
Two sub-experiments using commercial mineral water bottles.

What standard physics predicts
Gas has a density of approximately 1.2 kg/m3 . Water has a density of approximately 1000 kg/m3 .
That’s an 833× difference.
If SVEs were gas, two things must happen. First, during oscillation, gas would lag behind
the water: when water moves down, gas floats up relative to it; when water moves up, gas drifts
down relative to it. An 833× density mismatch produces visible phase separation within a single
oscillation cycle.
Second, gas rises. Always. In any liquid at rest, gas migrates to the surface. This is not
debatable—it is the definition of buoyancy.

The oscillation test proves that SVEs have the same density as water. The injection test proves it
by direct comparison: real gas rises; SVEs don’t.

Anomaly G2: SVEs Persist at Depth While Confirmed Gas Rises
The air injection control (C3, F3–F6) introduces a direct comparison: atmospheric air injected
via syringe into the same liquid, at the same moment, alongside SVEs already present. The
injected air rises to the surface within seconds. The SVEs remain at depth, stationary.
This eliminates all gas-phase hypotheses in a single observation: if SVEs were gas of
any composition (CO2 , N2 , O2 , or any mixture), they would rise. They do not. The only
interpretation consistent with stationary suspension at depth is ρSVE ≈ ρwater .
Anomaly G3: SVEs Survive CO2 Degassing
After vigorous shaking (C3, F1), both gas bubbles and SVEs are visible. After rest (C3, F2),
the gas has risen and dissipated—as gas does. The SVEs remain. This temporal persistence
eliminates the hypothesis that SVEs are transient gas nucleation sites: the gas has left, but the
structures persist. They are materially distinct from the dissolved gas that was temporarily
released by shaking.

Discussion
Density as Definitive Discriminator
Gas at atmospheric pressure has density ∼1.2 kg/m3 ; water has density ∼1000 kg/m3 . Even
compressed CO2 at the pressures present in commercial mineral water (∼3–4 atm) has density
∼6–8 kg/m3 —still > 100× less dense than water. No gas at any pressure achievable in a sealed
commercial bottle approaches water density.

The oscillation test provides a direct density measurement: objects with different density
than their surrounding fluid exhibit buoyancy-driven phase lag during oscillation. This is the
operational principle behind settling columns, centrifuges, and inertial separation. SVEs show
no such lag. Their density equals that of water.
Simultaneous Comparison: The Decisive Control
The air injection control (C3) elevates this from a negative result (absence of lag) to a positive
demonstration (simultaneous comparison): in the same container, at the same moment, gas rises
and SVEs do not. The falsification is not statistical—it is categorical. One population behaves
as gas; the other does not.
Convergence with Previous SVE Characterization
Combined with previous experiments documenting SVE properties:
• Spontaneous formation from homogeneous still water (Experiment 7, Sub-experiment A)
• Survival of ∼300 atm crystallization pressure during freezing (Experiment 7, Subexperiment B)
• Persistence inside ice after container removal (Experiment 7, Sub-experiment C)
• Concentric internal layers at 1000× magnification (Experiment 7, Sub-experiment C)
• Fracture lines under mechanical stress in frozen state (Experiment 7, Sub-experiment C)
• Directed migration toward heat sources across all phase states (Experiment 9)
• Reversible displacement upon heat removal (Experiment 9)
the present result completes the phenomenological characterization: SVEs are water-density
structures with internal organization, not gas bubbles with a simple gas-liquid interface.
Connection to Thermal Force Framework
SVE density equivalence with water is consistent with the thermal force framework established
in Experiments 1–9. If SVEs were gas, their response to thermal gradients (Experiment 9)
would be dominated by buoyancy and convection. Instead, their directed migration toward
heat—identical in wet, dry, and semi-wet samples—is consistent with ⃗Fthermal = −kT m∇T acting
on water-density matter.

Conclusion
Experiment 10 provides definitive empirical resolution to the question of SVE material identity.
Through two complementary experimental approaches—inertial density measurement via oscillation testing and simultaneous buoyancy comparison via air injection control—the gas bubble

hypothesis is categorically falsified. SVEs exhibit water-equivalent density (ρSVE ≈ ρwater ),
persist at depth while confirmed gas rises to the surface, and survive CO2 degassing.
The identity question is resolved: SVEs are material structures with the density of water and
internal organization. They are not gas.
