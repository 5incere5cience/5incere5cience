ABSTRACT
Classical thermodynamics and fluid mechanics dictate that a solid lipid emulsion,
upon undergoing phase transition to liquid via heat, should succumb to gravitational
force on an inclined plane, flowing downward along the path of least resistance. This
study presents empirical evidence contradicting this fundamental expectation. Using
controlled time-lapse photography (25 minutes) on a validated inclined plane, distinct
blocks of dairy butter were subjected to a localized, non-contact heat source (Tmax
<65∘C). We observed a consistent "Anti-Gravitational Climb," where the emulsion
climbed upward against the gravitational vector, directly towards the heat source.
Furthermore, we documented a "Vectorial Split" phenomenon, where a single sample
exhibited simultaneous upward (thermal attraction) and downward (gravitational pull)
movement, effectively mapping the event horizon where attractive thermal force
overcomes gravitational acceleration. These findings suggest that heat functions not
merely as a dispersive energy but as an active attractive force, offering a new
explanatory model for fluid dynamics, including the mechanics of boiling.

1. INTRODUCTION
1.1 The Classical Expectation In standard physics, applying heat to a solid lipid on an
incline causes melting (phase transition). Gravity then acts on the fluid mass, causing it
to flow downwards (F-gravity = m × g × sin(θ)). Any movement against gravity is typically
attributed to capillary action or Marangoni flows (surface tension gradients). However,
these forces generally affect surface layers, not the bulk transport of significant mass
against a steep incline.
1.2 The Hypothesis We posit that thermal energy acts as an attractive field. Matter
possesses an inherent tropism towards energy sources. In this model, the movement of
a fluid is a vector sum of Gravitational Pull (Down) vs. Thermal Attraction (Up).

3.1 The Anti-Gravitational Climb Upon reaching the phase transition (melting),
the lipid mass behavior was strictly dependent on its proximity to the heat
source. Unlike a standard gravitational flow, the butter blocks positioned within
the effective attractive radius exhibited a distinct flow up the incline, moving
directly against the gravitational vector towards the thermal focus.
Proximity Dependence: The movement was not uniform across the plane;
distal blocks remained stationary or unaffected, while proximal blocks
overcame gravity, confirming that the anti-gravitational force is spatially
localized and distance-dependent.
Observed Acceleration: While velocity was not quantitatively measured, visual
observation confirms a distinct acceleration of the lipid mass as it approached

the heat source. This behavior contradicts the constant deceleration expected
of an object moving against gravity without active propulsion.

4. DISCUSSION
4.1 Refuting "Melting and Sliding" If the standard model were correct, the reduction in
viscosity (phase transition from solid to liquid) would instantly result in a downward
slide due to gravity. The observation of mass climbing the slope proves that a force Fthermal exists and that F-thermal > F-gravity × sin(θ) in the observed zone.
4.2 Reinterpreting Boiling Dynamics This experiment provides a macro-scale model
for the mechanics of boiling water. We propose that the chaotic movement of boiling
water is not merely kinetic agitation, but a collective rush of water molecules being
attracted to the heat source at the bottom of the vessel.

This explains why, when the heat source is removed, the water immediately
"sits" or stills. The attractive force has ceased. If it were purely kinetic
momentum, the turbulence should persist longer due to inertia.

This experiment demonstrates that heat functions as an attractive vector capable of

laws. We conclude that fluids move towards heat not because of density differentials
alone (convection), but because Heat is an Attractor.
