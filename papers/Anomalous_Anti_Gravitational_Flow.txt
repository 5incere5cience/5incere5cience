Abstract
Classical thermodynamics and uid mechanics dictate that a solid lipid emulsion, upon undergoing phase transition to liquid via heat, should succumb to gravitational force on an inclined plane,
owing downward along the path of least resistance. This study presents empirical evidence contradicting this fundamental expectation. Using controlled time-lapse photography (25 minutes)
on a validated inclined plane, distinct blocks of dairy butter were subjected to a localized, noncontact heat source (T < 65°C). We observed a consistent maximum Anti-Gravitational Climb,
where the emulsion climbed upward against the gravitational vector, directly towards the heat
source. Furthermore, we documented a Vectorial Split phenomenon, where a single sample exhibited simultaneous upward (thermal attraction) and downward (gravitational pull) movement,
eectively mapping the event horizon where attractive thermal force overcomes gravitational
acceleration. These ndings suggest that heat functions not merely as a dispersive energy but
as an active attractive force, oering a new explanatory model for uid dynamics, including the
mechanics of boiling.

Introduction

1.1

The Classical Expectation

In standard physics, applying heat to a solid lipid on an incline causes melting (phase transition).
Gravity then acts on the uid mass, causing it to ow downwards [1, 2]. Any movement against
gravity is typically attributed to capillary action or Marangoni ows (surface tension gradients)
[3, 4]. However, these forces generally aect surface layers, not the bulk transport of signicant
mass against a steep incline.

1.2

The Hypothesis

We posit that thermal energy acts as an attractive eld. Matter possesses an inherent tropism
towards energy sources. In this model, the movement of a uid is a vector sum of Gravitational

Pull (Down) vs. Thermal Attraction (Up).

Results

3.1

The Anti-Gravitational Climb

Upon reaching the phase transition (melting), the lipid mass behavior was strictly dependent
on its proximity to the heat source.

Unlike a standard gravitational ow, the butter blocks

positioned within the eective attractive radius exhibited a distinct ow up the incline, moving
directly against the gravitational vector towards the thermal focus.

Proximity Dependence: The movement was not uniform across the plane; distal blocks
remained stationary or unaected, while proximal blocks overcame gravity, conrming that the
anti-gravitational force is spatially localized and distance-dependent.

Observed Acceleration: While velocity was not quantitatively measured, visual observation conrms a distinct acceleration of the lipid mass as it approached the heat source.

This

behavior contradicts the constant deceleration expected of an object moving against gravity
without active propulsion.

3.2

The Vectorial Split (The Tug of War)

Samples positioned at the threshold distance exhibited a distinct duality:

 The proximal portion of the mass sheared o and moved UP (Thermal Force >
Gravitational Force).

 The distal portion remained stationary or moved slightly DOWN (Gravitational
Force > Thermal Force).

 The furthest block (distal control) remained solid, conrming the localization of the
thermal eld.

3.3

Negative Evidence from Distal Zones

Crucially, the distal control blocks, positioned lower on the incline, remained entirely solid
throughout the experiment. This absence of phase transition in the lower zones serves as negative
evidence against downward thermal propagation. If heat were simply radiating or owing passively with gravity, the lower blocks should have eventually melted. Their solid state conrms
that the thermal force did not descend; rather, it was exclusively attractive, pulling only the
proximal mass upward while leaving the distal, lower mass gravitationally bound and thermally
isolated.

Discussion
Refuting Melting and Sliding

If the standard model were correct, the reduction in viscosity (phase transition from solid to
liquid) would instantly result in a downward slide due to gravity.

The observation of mass

climbing the slope proves that a force exists and that in the observed zone.

4.2

Reinterpreting Boiling Dynamics

This experiment provides a macro-scale model for the mechanics of boiling water [5]. We propose
that the chaotic movement of boiling water is not merely kinetic agitation, but a collective rush
of water molecules being attracted to the heat source at the bottom of the vessel. This explains
why, when the heat source is removed, the water immediately sits or stills. The attractive force
has ceased.

If it were purely kinetic momentum, the turbulence should persist longer due to

inertia.
