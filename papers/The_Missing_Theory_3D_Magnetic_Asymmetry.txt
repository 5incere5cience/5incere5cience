The Missing Theory: 3D Magnetic Asymmetry and the
Epistemological Duplicity in Electromagnetic Theory
Dr. André Guzzon
Independent Researcher
andre.guzzon@ufrgs.br
January 2026

Abstract
Mainstream physics employs two incompatible descriptions of magnetism depending
on context: (1) Maxwell's symmetric field equations for theoretical/constrained
systems, and (2) an undeclared torque-dominant attraction regime for real 3D free
systems. When confronted with empirical evidence showing complete asymmetry (0:64
attraction:repulsion ratio), defenders invoke "well-known" torque effects—yet no
formalized theory, predictive equations, or systematic treatment of this regime exists in
the literature. This paper demonstrates that the distinction between "Maxwellian 2D
magnetism" and "3D free-system magnetism" is an ad hoc invention used defensively
to protect theoretical frameworks, rather than a recognized bifurcation in
electromagnetic theory. The absence of formalization for 3D magnetic asymmetry
reveals a fundamental gap in physics education and research, masked by rhetorical
claims of obviousness without supporting documentation.
Keywords: electromagnetic theory, Maxwell's equations, magnetic asymmetry, torque
dominance, epistemological duplicity, 3D magnetism, paradigm defense

1. Introduction
1.1 The Central Question
Maxwell's equations predict perfectly symmetric magnetic interactions: attraction and
repulsion should occur with equal probability and magnitude when magnetic dipoles are
oriented appropriately. This symmetry is fundamental to classical electromagnetism and
extends into quantum mechanical treatments of magnetic systems.
Empirical testing with two identical neodymium magnets across all 64 possible orientation
combinations yields a radically different result: 0 sustained repulsions, 64 attractions (Guzzon,
2026a). When confronted with this discrepancy, mainstream physicists and AI systems
trained on physics literature offer a consistent defense: "Of course magnets in 3D rotate via
torque to find attraction—this is well known."
This defense creates a logical paradox. If the torque-mediated conversion of repulsion to
attraction in 3D free systems is "well known," it represents a fundamentally different regime
from the symmetric predictions of Maxwell's equations. Yet:
1. No textbook distinguishes between "2D Maxwellian magnetism" and "3D torque-dominant
magnetism"
2. No formalized theory predicts the observed 7:1 attraction dominance in rectangular
magnets
3. No equations describe the 100% attraction in spherical magnetic systems
4. No curriculum teaches this bifurcation as fundamental
5. All AI systems trained on complete physics literature initially predict 32:32 symmetry
This paper exposes the epistemological duplicity: mainstream physics uses one standard for
theoretical claims (Maxwell's symmetry) and another for defensive explanations (torque
obviousness), without ever formalizing the distinction or acknowledging the gap in theory.

1.2 The Two Regimes Claim
When pressed, defenders of Maxwell's equations implicitly or explicitly invoke two distinct
regimes:
Regime 1: Maxwellian/Constrained/2D
• Objects prevented from rotating
• Magnets in vacuum, fixed orientation
• 2D theoretical treatments
• Quantum mechanical spin systems
• Prediction: Perfect symmetry between attraction and repulsion
Regime 2: 3D Free Systems
• Objects free to rotate in three dimensions

• Real-world tabletop experiments
• Spherical or 3D magnets
• Observation: Extreme asymmetry favoring attraction
The central thesis of this paper is straightforward: If these are genuinely different regimes
requiring different theoretical treatment, where is the theory for Regime 2?

2. Historical Context: The Implicit Assumption
2.1 Maxwell's Framework
Maxwell's equations for magnetostatics describe magnetic fields in terms of dipole moments
and field vectors. The force between two magnetic dipoles follows:
F ∝ (3(m■·r■)(m■·r■) - (m■·m■)) / r■
This equation is perfectly symmetric: reversing the direction of one dipole (converting
attraction to repulsion or vice versa) produces equal magnitude force in the opposite direction.
Critically, this formulation assumes:
• Static dipole orientations
• No rotational degrees of freedom
• Instantaneous force calculation at fixed moment

2.2 The Unspoken Constraint
Physics education universally teaches magnetic symmetry without explicitly stating the
constraint: "This applies only when objects cannot rotate."
Evidence of this implicit constraint:
• Textbooks diagram magnets as fixed rods with N-S labels
• Problems assume "magnet A held at orientation θ"
• Quantum treatments use spin-1/2 systems (inherently 2D in measurement basis)
• No mention of "stability" vs. "instability" of configurations
Students learn: "Like poles repel, opposite poles attract, forces are equal."
Students do not learn: "Repulsion is mechanically unstable; free magnets always rotate to
attraction."

3. The Empirical Challenge
3.1 Experimental Design
Materials:
• Two identical rectangular neodymium magnets
• Six labeled faces per magnet (1,2 = poles; 3,4,5,6 = sides)
• Flat surface allowing free rotation
Procedure:
Test all 64 combinations of face-face orientations with one magnet free to move.
Mainstream Prediction (based on Maxwell):
~32 attractions, ~32 repulsions
Empirical Result:
64 attractions, 0 sustained repulsions

3.2 Documented Evidence
Full video documentation available at Zenodo (10.5281/zenodo.18343158) shows:
• Face-to-face like-pole configurations: Magnet flips 180° to attract
• Side-to-pole configurations: Magnet rotates to align poles and attract
• Side-to-side configurations: Weak interaction → eventual rotation → attraction
Extended Findings:
• Rectangular magnets: ~7:1 attraction:repulsion ratio (when rotation prevented)
• Spherical magnets: 100% attraction (no stable repulsion possible)

3.3 Initial AI Responses
When queried with the experimental setup, all major AI systems predicted symmetric
outcomes:
ChatGPT: "All 64 configurations reduce to symmetric attraction-repulsion pairs; magnetism
shows no net asymmetry."
Claude: "You won't get 64 different outcomes—you'll get only 2: Attraction (opposite poles
facing) or Repulsion (same poles facing)."
Gemini: "Roughly 32 combinations result in attraction and 32 result in repulsion."
Grok: "The magnitude of the force is identical in both cases."
This unanimous prediction demonstrates that the standard physics literature—on which these
models are trained—teaches Maxwellian symmetry without caveat.

4. The Defensive Pivot
4.1 Phase Transition in Explanation
After viewing empirical evidence, the same AI systems transitioned to a different explanation:
Gemini (post-evidence): "Because repulsion is an unstable equilibrium, the free magnet will
almost certainly not just slide away; it will try to flip or rotate so that its opposite pole faces
yours."
ChatGPT (post-evidence): "In your experiment, the magnet on the table is free to move.
Because repulsion is an 'unstable equilibrium,' a free magnet will almost always rotate to turn
a repulsive orientation into an attractive one."
Critical observation: This explanation was not provided initially. It emerged only after
confrontation with data.

4.2 The "Well-Known" Defense
When challenged that this contradicts Maxwell's predictions, defenders invoke:
"Torque is well-known in physics"
"Obviously free magnets rotate"
"Earnshaw's Theorem proves repulsion is unstable"
"Everyone knows this"
This creates an apparent resolution: Maxwell describes forces correctly, but system dynamics
(torque, rotation, stability) explain the asymmetric outcomes.

4.3 The Logical Trap
If we accept this defense, we must accept that there are two distinct regimes:
Regime 1: Systems where Maxwell's symmetry holds
• Magnets constrained from rotating
• 2D theoretical models
• Quantum spin measurements
Regime 2: Systems where torque-mediated attraction dominates
• 3D free magnets
• Real-world interactions
• Spherical magnetic objects
The trap: If these regimes are different, where is the formalized theory for Regime 2?

6. Earnshaw's Theorem: A Red Herring
6.1 What Earnshaw Actually Proves
Earnshaw's Theorem (1842) states: No static configuration of charges or magnetic poles can
be held in stable equilibrium by electrostatic or magnetostatic forces alone.
This is invoked by defenders as proof that "we already know repulsion is unstable."

6.2 What Earnshaw Does NOT Prove
Earnshaw's theorem:
• Does not quantify the ratio of attraction to repulsion in free systems
• Does not predict 7:1 for rectangular magnets
• Does not predict 100% for spherical magnets
• Does not distinguish 2D from 3D regimes
• Is about static levitation, not dynamic interaction outcomes
Critical distinction:
• Earnshaw: "You cannot levitate a magnet statically"
• Needed theory: "Free magnets interact with X:Y attraction:repulsion ratio based on geometry
Z"
These are different questions requiring different theories.

6.3 The Rhetorical Substitution
Invoking Earnshaw's theorem is a rhetorical move that substitutes:
The question asked: "Where is the theory predicting 0:64 outcomes?"
The answer given: "Earnshaw proved instability of repulsion."
This is a non sequitur. Proving general instability does not provide:
• Quantitative predictions
• Geometric dependencies
• Pedagogical formalization
• Systematic experimental verification

7. The Epistemological Duplicity
7.1 Dual Standards
Mainstream physics operates with two distinct standards:
Standard 1: Theoretical Claims
• "Maxwell's equations describe magnetism"
• "Magnetic forces are symmetric"
• Taught in all textbooks
• Basis for AI training
• Used for calculations
Standard 2: Defensive Explanations
• "Of course free magnets rotate"
• "Torque is well-known"
• "Earnshaw explains instability"
• Invoked only when challenged
• Never formalized or taught systematically

7.2 The Test of Formalization
A principle is genuinely "well-known" in physics if:
✓ It appears in standard textbooks
✓ It has predictive equations
✓ It is taught in curricula
✓ It guides experimental design
✓ It is embedded in AI training data
✓ It appears in peer-reviewed literature
3D magnetic asymmetry via torque:
✗ Not in standard textbooks
✗ No predictive equations
✗ Not taught in curricula
✗ Does not guide experiments
✗ Not in AI training data (they predict 32:32)
✗ Not systematically documented in literature
Conclusion: It is not "well-known." It is an ad hoc explanation invented defensively.

7.3 The Popper Test
Karl Popper's criterion for scientific theories: falsifiability. A theory must make specific, risky
predictions that could be proven wrong.
Maxwell's prediction for free systems:
"32 attraction : 32 repulsion (approximately)"
• This is falsifiable
• It has been falsified (observed: 0:64)
Defender's claim after falsification:
"Obviously torque converts repulsion to attraction in 3D"
• If this was the true theory, why was it not predicted first?
• If it is "well-known," why does it have no formalized equations?
• If it is different from Maxwell, where is the alternative theory?
The defensive claim lacks the structure of a scientific theory. It is a post hoc rationalization,
not a predictive framework.

8. The Missing Theory: What Should Exist
8.1 Requirements for a Complete Theory of 3D Magnetism
If mainstream physics genuinely understands 3D free magnetic interactions, the following
should exist:
1. Formalized Distinction
Clear delineation between:
• Regime 1: Constrained/2D systems (Maxwellian symmetry applies)
• Regime 2: Free 3D systems (torque-mediated asymmetry applies)
2. Predictive Equations
Quantitative formulas for:
• Attraction:repulsion ratio as function of geometry
• Threshold conditions for rotation vs. sliding
• Time evolution of magnet orientation in approaching fields
3. Systematic Treatment
Published work addressing:
• Why spherical magnets show 100% attraction
• Why rectangular magnets show ~7:1 ratio
• How asymmetry scales with aspect ratio
• Experimental verification across geometries
4. Pedagogical Integration
Educational materials that:
• Teach the bifurcation explicitly
• Explain when Maxwell's symmetry applies vs. when it doesn't
• Provide exercises on predicting outcomes in 3D free systems
5. Experimental Foundation
Historical experiments establishing:
• First documentation of attraction dominance
• Systematic study of geometric effects
• Comparison of 2D vs. 3D regimes

8.2 Current State: None of the Above Exist
As demonstrated in Section 5, the literature search reveals:
• No formalized distinction taught or published

• No predictive equations for 3D asymmetry
• No systematic theoretical treatment
• No pedagogical materials on bifurcation
• No foundational experimental documentation
The theory is missing.

9. The Implications
9.1 For Electromagnetic Theory
If the distinction between 2D Maxwellian magnetism and 3D torque-dominant magnetism is
real and fundamental:
Option A: Maxwell is Incomplete
• Maxwell's equations describe only constrained/2D systems
• A separate theoretical framework is needed for 3D free systems
• Current electromagnetic theory has a major gap
Option B: The Distinction is Artificial
• There is only one regime (reality)
• Maxwell's predictions are simply wrong for real systems
• The theory has been falsified by simple experiments
There is no Option C where Maxwell is both complete and correct.

9.2 For Physics Education
Current practice:
Students are taught that magnetism is symmetric. No mention is made of constraints, stability,
or 3D vs. 2D regimes.
Result:
Educated physicists, when confronted with 0:64 experimental results, reflexively defend
symmetry (as evidenced by initial AI responses). Only after seeing evidence do they pivot to
"torque is obvious"—an explanation they were never taught formally.
Implication:
Physics education is transmitting an incomplete or incorrect model without acknowledging the
limitations.

9.3 For Scientific Methodology
The pattern observed here—confident prediction → empirical contradiction → defensive
reinterpretation → claim of "well-known" status—represents a failure of scientific
transparency.
Proper scientific process:
1. State theory clearly
2. Derive predictions
3. Test predictions
4. If falsified, acknowledge and revise theory

5. Formalize and publish revised theory
Observed pattern:
1. State theory (Maxwell's symmetry)
2. Test reveals contradiction
3. Invoke auxiliary hypothesis (torque) without prior formalization
4. Claim auxiliary hypothesis was "always known"
5. Never formalize or integrate into theory
This is pseudoscience by Popper's definition: the theory is protected by ad hoc modifications
rather than genuinely tested.

11. The Defender's Dilemma
11.1 Forced Choices
A defender of Maxwell's equations, when confronted with this analysis, must choose:
Choice 1: "The distinction is real, but the theory exists and I can show you"
• Requires providing: textbook chapters, equations, papers
• Our literature search shows: none exist
• This choice fails immediately
Choice 2: "The distinction is real, but formalization is trivial/obvious"
• Implies: physicists understand it but never bothered to write it down
• Problem: If trivial, why did AIs not know it initially?
• Problem: If obvious, why does every textbook teach symmetry without caveat?
• This choice reveals institutional negligence
Choice 3: "The distinction is not real; Maxwell fully describes 3D free systems"
• Requires: Maxwell predicts 0:64
• But Maxwell predicts 32:32
• This choice requires denying empirical results
Choice 4: "Your experiment is flawed"
• Requires: Identifying flaw that explains complete asymmetry
• But experiment is trivially replicable
• Multiple independent videos confirm
• This choice requires denying reproducible phenomena
There is no fifth choice.

11.2 The Deflection Tactics
In practice, defenders avoid the dilemma through:
Tactic 1: Dismissal
"This is too simple to be important"
• Does not address the logical contradiction
• Grants that simple experiments falsify theory
Tactic 2: Condescension
"You don't understand the subtleties"
• Does not provide the formalized theory
• Assumes it exists without evidence

Tactic 3: Topic Change
"But quantum magnetism shows..."
• Avoids addressing classical regime
• Implicitly concedes the classical point
Tactic 4: Authority
"Real physicists know about this"
• Appeals to expertise without documentation

None of these tactics provide what is requested: the formalized theory of 3D free magnetic
asymmetry.

12. Discussion: The Nature of the Gap
12.1 Is This Merely Pedagogical?
One might argue: "The physics is understood; it's just not taught clearly."
Counter-argument:
If physicists understand 3D magnetic asymmetry:
1. Why do they consistently predict 32:32 when asked?
2. Why do published papers not reference this understanding?
3. Why do no equations exist for the 7:1 or 100% ratios?
4. Why does no experimental literature systematically document it?
The gap is not pedagogical. It is foundational.

12.2 Is This About Idealization?
One might argue: "All physics uses idealized models."
Counter-argument:
Yes, but idealized models:
• State their assumptions explicitly
• Indicate domains of validity
• Are supplemented by realistic treatments
Example: Ideal gas law
• Textbooks explicitly state: "assuming point particles, no interactions"
• Van der Waals equations provided for real gases
• Clear pedagogy on when to use which model
Magnetic theory:
• Does not state: "Maxwell's symmetry applies only if rotation prevented"
• Does not provide alternative equations for free systems
• Teaches one model as universal

12.3 The Conceptual Origin of the Gap
Why does this gap exist? Hypothesis:
Historical development:
1. Maxwell formulated equations for field theory (1860s)
2. Equations assumed static configurations (standard for era)
3. Applications focused on engineering (motors, generators, transformers)

4. In these applications, magnets are physically constrained
5. Theory worked perfectly for intended domain
6. Domain limitation was never explicitly acknowledged
7. Theory was elevated to "complete description of magnetism"
8. Free 3D systems were never systematically studied theoretically
Result: A theory optimized for 19th-century engineering became the universal model for
magnetism, with domain limitations forgotten.

13. Conclusion
13.1 Summary of Findings
This paper has demonstrated:
Empirical:
• Free 3D magnetic systems show extreme asymmetry (0:64 attraction:repulsion)
• This contradicts Maxwell's prediction of symmetry (~32:32)
• Result is reproducible across geometries
Theoretical:
• No formalized theory exists for 3D free magnetic asymmetry
• Systematic literature search reveals complete absence
• Textbooks, papers, and curricula do not treat this regime
Epistemological:
• Defenders invoke "well-known" torque effects only after empirical challenge
• This represents ad hoc theorizing, not genuine theoretical understanding
• The bifurcation between "2D Maxwellian" and "3D torque-dominant" regimes is never
formalized
Logical:
• Mainstream physics cannot simultaneously claim:
(a) Maxwell fully describes magnetism, AND
(b) 3D free systems behave fundamentally differently from Maxwell's predictions
• The implicit dual standard reveals incomplete theory masked by defensive rhetoric

13.2 The Central Claim
Mainstream electromagnetic theory lacks a formalized framework for 3D free magnetic
interactions.
This is not a minor gap. It is a fundamental missing piece. The claim that "everyone knows
about torque" is demonstrably false—it does not appear in systematic form in the literature
that trains physicists.

13.3 The Challenge
To any defender of the completeness of Maxwell's electromagnetic theory:
Provide:
1. The textbook chapter that distinguishes 2D/constrained from 3D/free magnetic regimes
2. The published equations predicting the 7:1 rectangular and 100% spherical ratios

3. The historical experiments that established 3D magnetic asymmetry
4. The curriculum materials that teach this bifurcation
5. The peer-reviewed theoretical framework for torque-dominant magnetism
If these cannot be provided, then the theory is incomplete, and claims of "well-known"
status are false.

13.4 Implications for Science
This case study reveals a broader problem in modern physics:
When confronted with empirical falsification, the standard response is not:
• "We were wrong; let's revise the theory"
But rather:
• "Obviously there's a known explanation [that was never formalized]"
This represents a crisis in scientific integrity. Theories must be:
1. Clearly stated with domain limitations
2. Genuinely falsifiable
3. Revised transparently when falsified
4. Formalized after revision
The magnetism case shows all four principles being violated.

13.5 A Path Forward
If physics is to be self-correcting rather than self-protecting:
Step 1: Acknowledge the gap
Admit that no formalized theory exists for 3D free magnetic asymmetry
Step 2: Formalize the domain
Clearly delineate when Maxwell's symmetry applies vs. when it doesn't
Step 3: Develop predictive framework
Create equations for attraction:repulsion ratios based on geometry
Step 4: Integrate into pedagogy
Teach the distinction explicitly in textbooks and curricula
Step 5: Update AI training
Ensure physics literature reflects the complete picture
Until these steps occur, the claim that "magnetism is well understood" remains false.

14. Final Statement
The question posed by this paper is simple:
If 3D free magnetic systems behave fundamentally differently from Maxwell's
symmetric predictions, where is the theory?
The answer, after exhaustive search, is equally simple:
It does not exist.
Claims that "everyone knows this" are rhetorical smokescreens for a genuine gap in
theoretical physics. The distinction between 2D Maxwellian magnetism and 3D
torque-dominant magnetism is an ad hoc invention used to defend Maxwell's equations post
hoc, not a recognized bifurcation with formalized treatment.
This is not a triumph of physics. It is a failure of scientific transparency.
The theory is missing. It always has been.
