2. Popper's Criterion of Demarcation
Karl Popper established the fundamental distinction between scientic and non-scientic
theories not through their truth content, but through their vulnerability to refutation [4].
A theory which is not refutable by any conceivable event is non-scientic. Irrefutability
is not a virtue but a vice. For Popper, scientic theories must be prohibitive they must
state clearly which events cannot happen.

A theory is scientic if: (a) it makes specic, risky predictions; (b) it prohibits certain
outcomes from occurring; (c) there exists a Black Swana single observation that would
refute it. A theory is non-scientic if: (a) it explains all possible observations; (b) contradictory evidence is reinterpreted as consistent; (c) it accommodates any outcome through
post-hoc rationalization.

2.2 A Note on Probabilistic Theories and Popper
Popper accepted probabilistic theories as scienticprovided they dene specic thresholds of improbability that, if violated, would count as falsication. A theory that predicts
event X will occur with probability 0.99 ± 0.01 is falsiable: if X occurs only 50% of the
time under controlled conditions, the theory is refuted. Statistical Thermodynamics, however, operates with extreme improbability without ever dening a threshold. No frequency
of anomalous observations is sucient to trigger falsication, because any deviation is accommodated as uctuation. This is the critical distinction: probabilistic theories can be
scientic; theories with undened probability thresholds that accommodate all outcomes
cannot.

5.3 The Formal Proof: Tautology and the Material Conditional
